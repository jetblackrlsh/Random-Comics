# Dream Caster

## Logline

A tired local access technician becomes the wielder of a cosmic TV remote that can broadcast forgotten story-forms into reality, forcing him to defend Earth and the Dream Realm from Nightmare incursions.

## Premise

Dream Caster follows Bob Carlson, a worn-down but sincere local access technician who believes public broadcasting is sacred because it gives ordinary people the power to bring their ideas, voices, and imagined worlds to life. After a supernatural storm connects Earth's broadcast signals to the Storyverse, Bob becomes the wielder of a futuristic TV remote glowing with purple Lucid Light.

The remote lets Bob tune into the Storyverse, a collection of cosmic bubble universes inside the Dream Realm. Every possible story exists somewhere in the Storyverse: aired, canceled, abandoned, forgotten, or never created. By channeling Lucid Light through the remote, Bob can temporarily cast story-forms into reality and transform into heroes, creatures, and genre archetypes from those lost worlds.

Across the Storyverse, a war is being fought between the Dream Guardians and the Nightmares. The Dream Guardians protect imagination, creativity, heroic possibility, and living stories. The Nightmares corrupt stories with fear, despair, static, and destructive imagination, twisting bubble universes into weapons.

As Dream Caster, Bob defends Earth and the Dream Realm from rogue channels, corrupted characters, lost-media monsters, and broken bubble universes leaking into reality. His mission is not only to fight evil, but to give forgotten dreams a signal.

## Tone

Earnest local-access superhero fantasy with storm-lit urban danger, surreal media magic, and hopeful devotion to ordinary people making stories. The series should feel grounded in real places and tired night-shift work, then erupt into cosmic purple dream energy, broadcast glitches, and impossible genre transformations.

## Recurring Visual Language

- Realistic candid photo-comic style with documentary phone-photo or handheld photojournalism framing.
- Local access spaces should look practical, underfunded, and loved: cable bundles, rack-mounted monitors, old bulletin boards, scuffed desks, loading docks, cameras, and broadcast dishes.
- Lucid Light appears as purple cosmic energy with clean star-specks, lensing, signal rings, and glowing remote-button trails.
- Nightmare corruption appears as black static, glitch blocks, dead-channel snow, warped signal lines, cracked pixels, and sickly light bleeding into real streets.
- Storyverse glimpses appear as floating translucent bubble universes containing impossible story worlds, viewed through broadcast monitors, Bob's eyes, or cosmic sky breaks.
- Bob's transformations should preserve his core heroic intent while changing genre silhouette: anime cosmic hero, giant robot puppet, rubber hose cartoon spring, and future story-forms.
- Caption boxes only. No speech bubbles or thought bubbles.

## Continuity Notes

- Bob Carlson believes public broadcasting matters because it lets ordinary people share ideas that would otherwise be ignored.
- The Dream Caster remote chooses Bob during the Channel Zero storm and becomes his recurring key item.
- Lucid Light empowers dreamers and lets Bob cast story-forms from forgotten, abandoned, canceled, or never-created stories.
- Nightmare corruption uses static, fear, despair, and destructive imagination to weaponize stories.
- The Storyverse is a collection of bubble universes inside the Dream Realm, and every possible story exists somewhere within it.
- The Dream Guardians and the Nightmares are at war across the Storyverse before Earth becomes involved.
- Bob's powers are new and instinctive in Issue #1; he survives by improvising through different story-forms rather than understanding the full system.
